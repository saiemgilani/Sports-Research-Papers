#### **Abstract** 

We study two important aspects of the home advantage for ice hockey. First we study team and season level data to uncover systematic variation in the home advantage across teams and over time. We find no systematic variation in home advantage across teams. Secondly, we use six seasons of game-level data to uncover sources of the home advantage. Our results show that additional rest for the home team has no impact on the win-loss probability. We find evidence that the home team’s rule advantage with regard to stick placement during face-offs helps to explain the home advantage, but that the home team’s rule advantage with regard to last line change opportunity does not. We find that the home advantage varies over the progression of the season, being the highest at the beginning and end of the season, and very small in the middle of the season. We also find that the home advantage is larger at the end of the season when the two teams are high in the standings. Some of these results are consistent with referee bias, but we are unable to show a causal relationship between referee bias and these results.

### **1.  INTRODUCTION** 

A well-known feature of sporting contests is the home-team advantage whereby the home team consistently wins more frequently than its opponent.   This is a natural outcome that benefits team owners since it promotes higher attendance and ticket sales.   All major team sports leagues guarantee each team play a balanced schedule where half of all games are played at home and half away, so that no team has a potential for a larger home advantage over the season.  In spite of this balance, the home advantage varies widely across teams and over time. During the past six seasons of the National Hockey League (NHL), the Boston Bruins had the lowest average home advantage of 2%, with a 50% win percentage at home and a 48% win percentage on the road.  In the 2010/11 season during which the Bruins won the Stanley cup, their winning percentage during regular season play was better on the road (59%) than playing at home (54%), giving it a home _dis_ advantage of 5%.  On the other hand, the Calgary Flames in the 2006/07 season had a substantially larger win percentage at home (73%) than on the road (30%). Even though the home-team advantage is an empirical regularity, each sporting contest is a random event, so why is it that the home team wins more frequently?  In this paper, we study two different aspects of the home advantage in ice hockey.  First, we ask whether the size of the home advantage varies systematically across teams and over time, by analyzing season-level data for all teams in the National Hockey League.  The 2010/11 season showed a historically small average home advantage of less than 4% whereas the prior 2009/10 season showed a rather large average advantage of over 12%.  Looking at team-specific data shows even more variation.  In this paper we seek to uncover persistent differences across teams and over time in the size of the advantage.    Secondly, we use game-level data to estimate win-loss probability models in an effort to uncover specific sources of the advantage.   A team’s performance, at home and on the road, reflects many aspects of play other than the home ice advantage. Although the existence of a home advantage is undisputed, there remains much speculation as to its sources.  Psychological factors including more rest for the home team, crowd support, referee bias and rules advantages are typically cited as sources of the home advantage. 

We use six seasons of game-level data for the NHL to examine how the home ice advantage varies across teams and over time.  We find that there is no systematic pattern to the home advantage.  It does not appear to differ significantly across teams, although it does show variation across seasons.  With regard to the sources of the home advantage, we find no evidence that rest between games has any effect on the home advantage.  We find that the home team’s rule advantage pertaining to face-offs is persistent across all six seasons of data, but that it does not explain all of the home advantage.  Furthermore, the

We analyze the effect of attendance on the likelihood that the home team wins and find that attendance has an ambiguous impact on the likelihood of winning.  Lastly, we conduct a number of analyses suggested by the literature on referee bias.   While we do not claim to find solid evidence of referee bias, we do find results that are consistent with it.  We separate games into games that end in regulation time with a one goal differential, those that end in overtime and those that end in a shootout.  We find that the size of the home advantage is larger for games that end in overtime than for those that end in regulation time with a one goal differential, yet we find there to be no home advantage in games that end in shootouts. ### **2.  REVIEW OF THE LITERATURE** 

Research into variations of the home advantage across teams and over time is not as plentiful as the research into the sources of the home advantage.  Harville and Smith (1994) find team-by-team variability in home advantage in college basketball to be relatively small.  This result was reinforced for professional basketball by Jones (2008) who finds team-specific home advantages in the National Basketball Association to lack statistical reliability.  On the other hand, Clarke (2005) finds that home-team advantage varies systematically across teams in Australian football and believes it is due to differences in crowd effects. Jamieson (2010) addresses many aspects of home advantage, including variation over time.  He suggests that the home advantage can be expected to rise with the recent increase in media coverage, which exploded with the growth of telecommunications.  However, he also suggests that the recent increase in turnover among the players on a team can diminish the size of the home advantage.  His only conclusion is that the home advantage was typically much larger in most sports prior to 1950. 

Although the presence of a home advantage is well established, unraveling the effect of the home crowd has been difficult, which in turn has resulted in a large body of research into the phenomenon.  Courneya and Carron (1992)

provide an extensive review of the literature, which was updated by Carron, Loughhead, and Bray (2005), and many researchers have drawn upon these reviews.  Popular explanations include more rest and more favorable rules for the home team and crowd support.  Entine and Small (2008) study the effect of rest on the home advantage for basketball, and find that more rest for the home team contributes to the home-court advantage.  Leard and Doyle (2011) use a single season of data for the National Hockey League (NHL), and find that none of the home advantage could be explained by the home team having more rest between games, but that a rule advantage for the home team with regard to face-off stick placements explained part of the advantage.  Although these results were fairly strong, they pertain to a single season of data. The impact of rules advantages for the home team have been studied in a number of sports, yet large differences in rules across sports makes a summary of the work difficult.  As for hockey, Liardi and Carron (2011) use pairwise correlations and find no significant correlation between face-off success for the home team and winning. 

In their book _Scorecasting,_ Moskowitz and Wertheim (2011) argue that most home-team advantage can be traced to referee bias. They rule out the effect of the crowd by showing that the home and away teams are equally likely to score during shootouts in hockey and free-throw percentages in basketball.  The fact that the home team doesn’t perform any better than the away team in these situations, they argue, is evidence against the influence of the home crowd. However, the home crowd can have an indirect effect through the behavior of the referees.  Pettersson-Lidbom and Priks (2010) find that referees punish away teams more harshly than home teams when games are played in front of fans compared to games played in empty arenas.  Garicano et al. (2005) find evidence of referee bias in European soccer, but that it was dependent on the closeness of the game.  In close games, referees showed a larger bias in favor of the home team by adding more minutes for injury time when the home team was behind by one goal, but not when the home team was behind by more than one goal, the extra time allowing the home team a better chance to score.

The explanatory variables _Xjit_ are chosen to capture the differences between the performances of the teams at home and on the road.  These include differences in home and away goalie save percentage ( _DGSVPCT_ ), power play opportunities ( _DPPLAY_ ), possession efficiency ( _DPOEFF_ ), and face-offs won ( _DFACE_ ). #### **5.3 EFFECT OF ATTENDANCE** 

The effect of the crowd as a contributing factor behind the home advantage was dismissed by Moskowitz and Wertheim (2011) by citing the observation that the performance of the home team is no better than the away team for free throws in basketball and for shootouts in hockey. This conclusion is not necessarily enough to debunk the hypothesis of the influence of the crowd, since a free throw in basketball involves pure shooting ability and no defensive ability on the part of the opposing team.  As for hockey, a shootout occurs after the two teams have played 60 minutes of regulation play and 5 minutes of overtime play.  In this case, the shootout only captures the ability of the goalie versus a single offensive player.  In fact, Smith and Groetzinger (2010) find a positive impact of attendance on the home-team advantage in baseball.  We test for the effect of attendance on the probability that the _home_ team wins by reorganizing our data such that the dependent variable is a dichotomous variable indicating whether the home team wins or loses.  The independent variables are similar to those in Table 5 except that the performance differentials are constructed as the difference between the home and the away teams’ performance.  We exclude the home dummy, which is no longer needed, and include an attendance variable ( _PERATT_ ) that measures attendance as a percentage of capacity.  We hypothesize that a larger crowd bodes well for the home team, and we expect a positive coefficient on _PERATT_ .  Of course, it could be that the crowd effect is not adequately measured by _PERATT_ , but given our data it is our only measure for the crowd. 

This win-loss model for the home team can be used to pick up the effect of the home team’s last line change rule advantage.   As discussed earlier, the home team is granted rules advantages in the NHL, such as the face-off stick placement

_Submission to Journal of Quantitative Analysis in Sports_ 

rule and also the opportunity to make the last line change before play resumes. The number of these last-line change opportunities aligns with the number of play stoppages, because the last line change opportunity occurs when play resumes. We use the total number of face-offs ( _TFACE_ ) in the game as a proxy for the number of these last line change opportunities, since the resumption of play begins with a face-off.<sup>8</sup> If the last line change opportunity is beneficial to the home team, we would expect that more opportunities for changing the line would increase the probability of winning, holding the other covariates constant. 

The win-loss probability model with attendance as an explanatory likely suffers from endogeneity bias as discussed by Smith and Groetzinger (2010), whereby attendance not only may improve the chances of the home team winning, but also a higher expectation of a win will attract a larger crowd.  That is, home teams who are expected to win will attract a larger crowd so a positive coefficient on attendance in the win-loss probit model cannot be interpreted as evidence that the crowd improves the probability of winning.  We replicate their solution whereby we use instrumental variables estimation to mitigate the endogeneity bias. For instruments, we use the day of the week and a set of team effects. We then add the differential performance variables that were used in the previous probit models. In column 1 we find that attendance has a small but significant positive effect on the probability of winning although differential days off does not.  When we add the differential performance variables, the impact of attendance becomes statistically insignificant.  The _PERATT_ variable is highly skewed, with several teams at 100% capacity for all games.  It could well be that _PERATT_ doesn’t provide enough variation after the endogenous variation is removed by the instrumental variables.  Lastly, we find that total face-offs, our proxy for the home team’s advantage of having the last line change opportunity before the resumption of play, has no measureable impact on the probability that the home team wins the game. ### **6.  CONCLUSION** 

In this paper we have analyzed the home advantage in the NHL from two different perspectives.  Using season-level data we find no systematic variation in the home advantage across teams.  The advantage varies over time, but there is little evidence to suggest that it is anything but random.  In order to uncover the sources of the home advantage, it is necessary to use game-level data.  We use six seasons of game-level data to study the oft cited sources of better rest, rules advantages, crowd effects and referee bias.   Our results show that more rest before games has no effect on the win-loss probability for the home team.  We find good evidence that the rules advantage for the home team regarding stick placement during face-offs contributes to the home advantage, but we find no evidence that having the last line change opportunity benefits the home team.  We found no evidence of a crowd-size effect on the win-loss probability.  As for referee bias, we found some evidence that is consistent with referee bias, but we cannot argue that it is caused by referee bias.  This was evident in our finding that home teams are no more likely to win an overtime game that goes to a shootout, but they are more likely to win a game that ends in overtime compared to a game that ends in regulation with a one goal differential.  In order to flush out this effect, we would require more micro data, such as period by period data. Information about the awarding of penalty minutes during overtime, could be used to study referee bias. Our finding that the home team has a larger advantage when there is more at stake with regard to the playoffs is consistent with a number of hypotheses, including referee bias, but also consistent with the psychological effects of crowd support.  What is needed is a clever identification strategy akin to the empty stadium natural experiment that was studied in soccer by PetterssonLidbom and Priks (2010).
