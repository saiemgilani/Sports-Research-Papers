### **Abstract** 

This study analyzes how the efficiency of basketball players depends on experience, player’s nationality and team type. Using data for players in the Turkish Basketball League (TBL) from 1997 to 2010, we first show that efficiency of players initially increase with experience, but after a certain level decrease with it. Second, foreign players are on average more talented than native players. Third, the experience–efficiency link is not valid for foreign players indicating that more experience does not affect efficiency. Fourth, efficiency of foreign players in top-teams is no different than that of foreign players in regular (non-top) teams; whereas, native players in topteams are more efficient than native players in other teams. Finally, in the presence of foreign player quota, top-teams hire the maximum number of foreign players allowed, while financially constrained regular teams can afford less foreign players. Consequently, under foreign player quota the quality of native players and the extent to which a team can pay for additional foreign players determine the team success. 

**KEYWORDS:** efficiency, basketball, foreign player quota, experience, panel data

### **1. Introduction** 

Individual talent is a necessary ingredient for a successful career in sports, although it is not sufficient by itself. What determines success is how effectively and efficiently an athlete combines his talent with mental skills in pursuit of constant improvement of game capacity. In basketball sense, although talent is a nature’s gift, ability to fully internalize the philosophy of the game comes with practice and maturity. Hence, experience is a key factor for efficient use of individual talent in the name of collective success of the team. 

In economic terms, efficiency is simply the comparison of what is actually produced with what can be achieved by consuming the same amount of resources. In sports, the resources available to an athlete are physiological aspects like energetic ability, flexibility and muscular strength; technical aspects like the precision in performing a fundamental movement and mental aspects like understanding and applying tactics. Hence, how good these resources are utilized up to their potential determines individual efficiency in sports. Although the set of abilities shaping up the efficiency is not directly observable, it is reflected in game statistics which can be considered as indicating implied efficiency. Therefore considering individual game statistics like points, assists, turnovers in basketball as available inputs, it is possible to come up with an efficiency measure. In its simplest form, efficiency of a basketball player can be defined as the sum of positive (such as points, assists, rebounds) and negative inputs (such as field goals missed) utilized for producing a win for the team. 

Efficiency of a player is a reflection of his quality, and therefore, an important indicator of his market value as well. Considering skill abundance and comparative advantage along with international mobility, one may expect all the domestic leagues to be filled with players from a certain set of nations. However, existence of foreign player quotas pushes the demand for domestic players up along with their market value. In this respect it is of great value to determine how efficient foreign and native players are and whether foreign players are in fact more efficient than native ones. The answers to these questions have important economic implications for what type of players and from which source to hire under foreign player quotas. 

Using data for players in the Turkish Basketball League (TBL) from 1997 to 2010, in this paper we first show that efficiency of players initially increase with experience, but after a certain level decrease with it. Second, we show that foreign players are on average more talented than native players. Third, the experience– efficiency link is not valid for foreign players indicating that more experience

### **2. Overview of the Literature and Methodology** 

### **2.1. An Overview of the Literature** 

In today’s global environment many goods and services, including athletic talent, are traded between countries. Basketball is not an exception as players can move freely around the world to a large extent. Yet, national basketball organizations are allowed to establish quotas for the number of foreign players that domestic teams can contract. For example, Falcous and Maguire (2005) report that while most European leagues allowed only two foreign players, five players were allowed in Britain in late 1990s. Heterogeneity among the quota numbers of national organizations is still present in European basketball, even though quotas are being relaxed and the number of foreign players is increasing. Alvarez et al. (2011) report that after 1995, the average number of foreign basketball players in European leagues increased from 2.46 to 5.62 per team in 2007. As of 2010 in Russia, there is a requirement of seven domestic players per team and a minimum of one domestic player on the court during the play of the game. In Greece only two non-EU players are allowed. In Turkey five foreign players are allowed per team in a game roster, although at most three of them can play on the court at the same time. In Lithuania and Germany there are no restrictions on the use of foreign players. 

The main argument behind the imposition of foreign player quota in sports is the possible negative spillover effects of foreign players on the development of local talent and on the performance of the national team. Baur and Lehmann (2007) report that as the number of imported players increases, the foreigners capture more of the game time and national players accumulate less experience. Verbon (2008), stressing the implications for domestic player development, shows that without any quota rules, small leagues will lose the incentive and the motivation to train local talents and the outcome will be the undersupply of local talent. On the other hand, Alvarez et al. (2011) find that increased number of

Protectionism through imposing a quota has implications on the demand for and the competitiveness of domestic players in this case, and on the quality of the domestic league in general. The existence of foreign player quotas increases the demand and the wages of native players. Lang, Rathke and Runkel (2009) investigate the effects of restrictions on foreign players with a model based on contract theory. They show that when the restriction on the number of foreign players is binding, the competitive balance, wages for local players and profits of the clubs are higher compared to the case where restrictions are not binding. However, Flores, Forrest and Tena (2010) show that liberalization could improve the competitive balance depending on the size of the talent pool. Overall, the direction of the spillover effect of foreign players is rather undecided in the literature. 

In sports economics literature studies on the contribution of players to team level efficiency and productivity, it is typical to use a production function approach. Similarly, Teramoto and Cross (2010) examine the contributions of efficiency and related factors to winning games in both regular season and the playoffs in NBA. Bai (2009) examines how environmental factors affect the individual efficiency of players from NBA. These studies however do not make comparison between the efficiency of certain type of players. Specifically, in sports economics literature, what lacks is the comparison of efficiency of foreign and domestic players under the presence of foreign player quotas. 

Another interesting issue to explore is how the level of experience affects efficiency and how this relation differs for foreign and native players when there is a foreign player quota. In the labor economics and population studies literature, there is a considerable amount of evidence on the link between age/experience and productivity. For instance, Skirbekk (2004), providing a survey, documents that the productivity profile of a working person as a function of age is initially low, then increasing, and later decreasing as the person’s job related skills start to deteriorate. Skirbekk (2008), in addition, finds that the shape of this relation changes with the abilities demanded in the market. Marchetti (2002) shows that for professions requiring both physical and mental skills such as for athletes,

In addition to player efficiency, financial strength is also an important factor for the success of a team. Generally, teams in European sports leagues are more concerned with maximizing success in winning rather than profit. Teams in smallsized leagues have difficulties in raising revenues from outside sources and are generally financed by locally-generated revenues and the league’s governing body. The fragile financial status of teams and their focus on winning may harm the size and quality of the league, and financially strong teams are more likely to succeed by hiring more talented native and foreign players. 

### **2.2. Overview of the Methodology** 

This study analyzes how the efficiency of basketball players depends on experience, player nationality and team type. In order to analyze this one may use data for a single team in one season or for all the teams in one season, which yields a cross section data set. Instead, more generally one may use data for all the teams in all the seasons available, where in this case, we have a panel dataset including observations for a large number of players over many seasons. An advantage of the panel data is that it provides tools for handling the unobserved heterogeneity –the unobserved player specific factors affecting their efficiency in our context. Panel data can be modeled by use of fixed or random effects models. A fixed effects model assumes that the unobserved individual heterogeneity is correlated with covariates of the model and uses the within group estimator in order to deal with this type of unobserved effect. In contrast, random effects model assumes that the individual heterogeneity is random and the model is estimated by a feasible generalized least squares procedure. Baltagi (2008) provides an extensive presentation of the panel data estimation methods. In the case of basketball players, unobserved individual effect may be attributed to inherit ability or talent of a player, which is obviously correlated with covariates such as team membership or the number of playing minutes. In the case of a panel data with unobserved individual effects matter, the use of the fixed effects estimator more appropriate for basketball players, as reported in many studies including Lee and Berri (2008), Hofler and Payne (2006).

Fixed effects estimator can alternatively be obtained by the Least Squares Dummy Variable (LSDV) approach, where a dummy variable for each individual player is included in the estimation. In our context, the LSDV approach is more useful as the estimates of unobserved individual effects (individual talent) are also provided. Although the coefficients of covariates in the LSDV model are consistent, the coefficients of fixed effects may be inconsistent for small time periods. However, this methodology is still valid since we are interested only in the relative comparisons of unobserved talents of different types of players. Apart from the individual unobserved effects, team and season specific factors may also affect the efficiency-experience relation. Thus in order to control for these factors, dummy variables for each team and season are also included in the estimation procedure.

### **3.1. Turkish Basketball League** 

Basketball is the second most followed sport in Turkey after football, yet it receives increasingly more public attention from year to year. Recently, in the last World Championship, 2010, the Turkish national basketball team marked an historical accomplishment by winning the silver medal. Currently, Turkish national men’s team ranks 6<sup>th</sup> on the FIBA’s (International Basketball Federation) latest rankings. 

The Turkish Basketball League (TBL) was formed officially in 1966 and is managed by the Turkish Basketball Federation. Over this 44 year-period, more than 60 different teams competed in the league, while only 11 of them have won the league title. Efes Pilsen Spor Kulübü (Efes Pilsen), has recently dominated the league with 13 championships and won the Korac Cup in 1996, first European title won by a Turkish team at any team sports. In the last decade, Efes Pilsen, Ülkerspor and Fenerbahçe&Ülker are the only teams winning the title, while Beşiktaş, Galatasaray and Türk Telekom are among the top performers in the league. TBL currently ranks 6<sup>th</sup> in domestic league rankings of ULEB (Union of European Leagues of Basketball). 

TBL is played with 16 teams. At the end of the regular season, the first 8 teams advance to play-offs, while the last two are relegated to the second division. The champion is determined after a best-of-seven final series. Although the playoffs determine the champion, the standings in regular season play an important role for the play-off match-ups, thus the regular season has a similar competitive level as the play-offs. It is important to discuss how the efficiency is defined and how it differs from productivity. Individual productivity of a basketball player refers to the final outcomes like points scored, rebounds grabbed, assists made and shots blocked. Efficiency, on the other hand, is about how effectively a player produces this output. Using individual statistics, as a general practice for efficiency, observed positive outcomes are adjusted for turnovers and number of shots missed. This study also uses this generic definition of an efficiency index: Efficiency = [Points + Rebounds + Assists + Steals + Blocks - (Field Goals Attempted - Field Goals Made) - (Free Throws Attempted - Free Throws Made) - Turnovers]. This efficiency index is widely used officially by professional leagues including the NBA, TBL and many European basketball leagues as reported in Martínez (2010). Moreover, this index is also used in various studies such as Coates and Oguntimein (2010), Groothuis, Hill and Perri (2009), Rice and Sen (2008) among others. A general property of the efficiency index should be addressed. Efficiency is a cumulative index and depends on the number of minutes a player plays in a game. The relationship between efficiency and minutes played is not clear. Initially, playing more minutes may increase the efficiency, but after a threshold, it may also decrease the efficiency if the player gets tired and accumulates mistakes. Sampaio et al. (2010) show that even though the playing time matters, errors made is the most important factor distinguishing important and less important basketball players, in other words, efficient and less efficient players. Moreover, Berri and Bradbury (2010) document that the correlation between NBA efficiency index (not corrected for minutes) and Game Score index (an efficiency index corrected for minutes) is 0.99 for a sample of NBA players from 2007 to 2009. Therefore, in this study we will focus on the generic efficiency index, unadjusted for playing time, as described above. 

### **3.3. Model** 

In this study, we consider a model where player efficiency is a function of experience and number of games played in a season. Efficiency is expected to increase first and later to decrease with experience after a certain level, where age of a player is used as a proxy for experience. To capture the nonlinear relationship between efficiency and age, the variables age and age-squared are included in the model. Using age as a proxy for experience seems to be a fair assumption in the case of basketball players, as most players start their careers at a similar age in early school years. Bai (2009) also follows this approach. The number of games a player played in a season is included in the model as well, since playing practice also affects efficiency. 

A player’s efficiency depends on the team and season in which he plays, as well as his talent. Efficiency of a player is also related to the performance of teammates. For example, one player who takes many shots may have a negative impact on other players’ efficiencies, and a player’s efficiency may be enhanced on a team with better players. Idson and Kahane (2000) report that in National Hockey League team characteristics affect the performance of individual players; while Torgler and Schmidt (2007) show that teammate characteristics affect the individual performance in soccer. ### **4. Concluding Remarks** 

The limitations on international mobility of players via foreign player quotas have important implications in the context of the study. First, it increases the demand for native players, even though they are not necessarily as efficient as foreign players. As foreign players are mobile, it is easy to sign a new player in place of an inefficient one, and therefore the turnover rate of foreign players is high. On the other hand, as the market for native players is quite tight, it is not easy to replace an inefficient player, and eventually, there is always demand for less efficient players. Second, regular (non-top) teams need to spend more resources on domestic player development for survival given that native players must be used and experienced natives are expensive to employ. Third, the finding that foreign players perform almost equally efficiently in top and regular (non-top) teams implies that regular teams are successful at recruiting foreign players that could match the efficiency of foreign players playing at top-teams. This research suggests additional questions for future study. The effects of the changes in foreign player quota on the player choice of teams and whether foreign players harm the domestic player development should be analyzed with more detailed data. Another question for future study is how to strengthen the financial status of teams to improve their sustainability and the competitive balance of the league.
