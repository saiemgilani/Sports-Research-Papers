## Comparing Team Selection and Seeding for the 2011 NCAA Men’s Basketball Tournament 

### Kathy L. Gray and Neil C. Schwertman 

### **Abstract** 

The men’s NCAA basketball tournament is a popular sporting event often referred to as “March Madness.” Each year the NCAA committee not only selects but also seeds the tournament teams. Invariably there is much discussion about which teams were included and excluded as well as discussion about the seeding of the teams. In this paper, we propose an innovative heuristic measure of team success, and we investigate how well the NCAA committee seeding compares to the computer-based placements by Sagarin and the rating percentage index (RPI). For the 2011 tournament, the NCAA committee selection process performed better than those based solely on the computer methods in determining tournament success. 

**KEYWORDS:** NCAA, men's basketball, seeding, RPI, Sagarin

### INTRODUCTION 

Each March the NCAA sponsors basketball tournaments featuring teams from various universities to play for the National Championship.  While the women’s Division I tournament is growing in popularity, it still is overshadowed by the men’s event.   The tournaments often sprout office pools and are frequently referred to as “March Madness”. Furthermore, the men’s tournament has been the subject of several statistical analyses, see for example, Carlin (1996), Caudill and Godwin (2002), Schwertman et al. (1991), Schwertman et al. (1996), Smith and Schwertman (1999) or West (2008). The focus of these papers is to predict team’s success in the tournament based on their seed position or other measures of team strength. In this paper, however, instead of concentrating on predicting success, the interest is how well a team’s tournament success correlates with their seeding by the NCAA committee and two seed placements schemes based on computer ratings. 

In designing the tournament, the NCAA uses a panel of experts to select and seed the teams to compete for the National Championship.  Division I conference champions or their conference tournament winners are automatically included in the tournament to give a broad representation of the basketball talent throughout the United States. Besides the automatic qualifiers, about half of the tournament teams are “at-large” selections based on their record. Of these, there are always several teams which are “on the bubble”, that is, inclusion in the tournament is questionable and could go either way. To include more “bubble” teams, in 2011 the NCAA expanded the field to 68 teams. Sixty teams were assigned a seed position while the other eight teams played a preliminary game, four played for a 16<sup>th</sup> seed, two each played for an 11<sup>th</sup> and 12<sup>th</sup> seed.  These preliminary games are considered as an additional regular season game where the eight teams are competing for inclusion in the tournament. Thus the final 64 teams are now on an equal basis and only the play of the final 64 teams after the preliminary games are used in the analysis. Following the selection committee announcement of the teams and seeding there is a great deal of controversy about which teams were included and excluded and also the seed placements. 

The objectives of this paper are first to propose an innovative simple heuristic measure of a team’s success in the tournament, second, to examine how well the seed placement correlates with the new measure of team’s success and finally to determine if seeding based only on the computer ratings correlate better with team success. 

The computer ratings used are the Sagarin and RPI scores. The Sagarin computer ratings which appear regularly in _USA Today_ is by Jeff Sagarin and incorporates two rating systems. The first, the Elo Chest used for rating chess players is adapted to basketball and uses only wins and losses while the second,

### MEASURING TOURNAMENT SUCCESS 

The easiest method of determining tournament success is to count the number of wins. Thus, a top seed win over a bottom seed would count the same as the other way around. In addition, the relative strength of the team and the closeness of the game are not considered and hence there is no measure of the relative successes within the groups with no wins, one win, etc.. A second simple method is to count a first round win 1 point, a second round 2 points, etc.. This assumes that as the tournament progresses the competition is tougher. Similar to just counting wins, this method does not consider the closeness of the score nor provides the relative success within the teams grouped by number of wins and, at best, only roughly incorporates the quality of the competition. 

As an alternative, we suggest a heuristic measure of success.  Specifically, the number of points a team scores in a game is an indication of how well they did

on offense and the inverse of what their opponent scored is a measure of how well they did on defense. Thus, the ratio of their score to the opponent’s score incorporates the teams overall performance in that game. This still does not measure the quality of the competition. To make this adjustment we suggest using the z-scoring introduced by Schwertman et. al (1991) to measure the relative strengths of the teams.  Using their procedure, the average percentiles for seeds 1 through 16 are z-scored.  For example, the top seeds are ranked 1, 2, 3 and 4 and the median of these numbers is 2.5.  Since there are 345 teams in Division I and using the continuity correction, the percentile is 100(1–2.5/345.5) = 99.276%tile which equates to Z=2.445. For the number 2 seeds the rankings are 5, 6, 7 and 8, the median is 6.5, the percentile is 100(1-6.5/345.5) = 98.119%tile and Z=2.079. Table 2 gives the z-score for each seed.

This measure may still give the losing team more success points than the winning team but the winning team’s advantage is that they continue playing and are afforded the opportunity to acquire more points. For example, in the first round Kentucky, a 4 seed, defeated Princeton, a 13 seed, 59 -57 and their success points are (59/57) 1.053 = 1.08995 while Princeton’s success points are (57/59) 1.728= 1.66942. As illustrated, the measure of success places the stronger seeds at a distinct disadvantage in the early rounds since they face weaker opposition and the z-scores of the competition are small but, as in the case of Kentucky, the opportunity for further play allows for future success. ### CONCLUDING COMMENTS 

While it is unlikely that the Selection Committee will ever achieve perfection, during the first year of the 64 team format (1985) there were no upsets (lower seeds beating upper seeds) in the Eastern region which is considered perfect placement for this region.  This analysis of 2011 tournament data shows that the Selection Committee in 2011 was quite effective in the seeding of the tournament and that basing the seeding entirely on computer ratings was not an advantage since, of the three placements, the NCAA seeding had the strongest correlation with team success points and the fewest upsets.  The incorporation of some subjectivity into their seeding appears to be advantageous.  The committee is able to make an adjustment, for example, if a key player is injured or unable to play for some reason.  From this analysis of the data for the 2011 tournament, there is ample evidence that NCAA selection committee was proficient in the selection and seeding of the tournament teams.
